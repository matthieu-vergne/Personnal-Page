Dictionary Definitions

- Relevant from Cambridge Dictionaries: - connected with what is happening or being discussed seems rather neutral by establishing a mere relation but, because something unrelated to the matter at hand can be formally related to it with the relationship is unrelated to, everything can be related to anything. This means that we should not speak about a mere relation but something stronger. - correct or suitable for a particular purpose highlights this aspect, establishing that the relation should be supported by the purpose of the matter at hand, thus a goal-driven perspective. - Relevant from Oxford Dictionaries: - closely connected or appropriate to what is being done or considered. confirm this notion of some relations being more valuable than others because they are closer. A distance for which the dimension should be chosen depending on the need, bringing again a goal-driven perspective. - Relevant from Merriam-Webster: - having significant and demonstrable bearing on the matter at hand highlights again the valuable aspect of the element through its significance, which should be demonstrable rather than merely stated. Two aspects which support again the need to establish a goal-driven perspective from which such a value can be inferred. - affording evidence tending to prove or disprove the matter at issue or under discussion, a particular case where the goal is to prove something, such that a relevant element is an element which can be used in this proof. - having social relevance, a particular case where the goal relates to social relationships, such as people interests or needs. Despite its circularity, this social aspect is an important one when speaking about the expertise of people.

Grice described these expectations in terms of a Cooperative Principle and maxims of Quality (truthfulness), Quantity (informativeness), Relation (relevance), and Manner (clarity), which speakers are expected to observe (Grice 1961, 1989: 368–72). - We share Grice's intuition that utterances raise expectations of relevance, but question several other aspects of his account, including the need for a Cooperative Principle and maxims, the focus on pragmatic contributions to implicit (as opposed to explicit) content, the role of maxim violation in utterance interpretation, and the treatment of figurative utterances. - History in several stages: A detailed version was published in Relevance: Communication and Cognition (Sperber and Wilson 1986a, 1987a, b) and updated in Sperber and Wilson (1995, 1998a, 2002) and Wilson and Sperber (2002). - Central claim: expectations of relevance raised by an utterance are precise and predictable enough to guide the hearer toward the speaker's meaning. - Aim: explain in cognitively realistic terms what these expectations amount to, and how they might contribute to an empirically plausible account of comprehension. - Relevance: - Relevance is a potential property of observable phenomena, thoughts, memories, and conclusions of inferences. Any external stimulus or internal representation which provides an input to cognitive processes may be relevant to an individual at some time. - An input is relevant to an individual when its processing in a context of available assumptions yields a positive cognitive effect. - A positive cognitive effect is a worthwhile difference to the individual's representation of the world . False conclusions are cognitive effects, but not positive ones, as opposed to true conclusions, which makes the latter potentially relevant as opposed to the former. - Relevance is a matter of degree, or more precisely a matter of comparison, based on cognitive effect and processing effort. Other things being equal, the greater the positive cognitive effects achieved by processing an input, the greater its relevance will be.. Other things being equal, the greater the processing effort required, the less relevant the input will be.. - Cognitive Principle of Relevance: Human cognition tends to be geared to the maximization of relevance, not because we have a choice in the matter but because of the way our cognitive systems have evolved, as a result of constant selection pressures toward increasing efficiency. - Cognitive effects: - Contextual implication: a conclusion deducible from input and context together, but from neither input nor context alone. For example, on seeing my train arriving, I might look at my watch, access my knowledge of the train timetable, and derive the contextual implication that my train is late - Strengthening, revision, or abandonment of available assumptions: For example, the sight of my train arriving late might confirm my impression that the service is deteriorating, or make me alter my plans to do some shopping on the way to work. - According to relevance theory, an input is relevant to an individual when, and only when, its processing yields such positive cognitive effects. (the list is non exhaustive, though) - Communication: - Informative intention: The intention to inform an audience of something. Alone, it is not sufficient to ensure the establishement of a communication, because the resulting action may lead the information to be provided by the communicator but not grasped by the audience. For example, putting one's empty glass in the vision field of the audience to ask for a refill, while hoping that the audience will notice and understand it without direct interaction with the communicator. - Communicative intention: The intention to inform the audience of one's informative intention. The resulting action would lead the communicator to ensure that the audience grasp the information for proper reaction. For example, calling the audience before to show the empty glass. - Ostensive-inferential communication: acting in order to fulfil both an informative intention with a related communicative intention. It involves the use of an ostensive stimulus, designed to attract an audience's attention and focus it on the communicator's meaning. Higgins, E.
